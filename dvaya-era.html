The Dvaya Era

Reigns of Mitraman and Sumant · ~100–20 BCE · Constitutional founding epoch

The Dvaya era (~100–20 BCE) comprises the reigns of Mitraman and Sumant. Medieval scholars coined the term Dvaya (Sanskrit: the Pair) to describe the two reigns as a single constitutional epoch.

Background

By the seventh or eighth generation after Avantian independence (180 BCE), prosperity had created a political problem: merchant families had accumulated sufficient wealth to maintain private armed naval escorts, transforming them from commercial actors into political ones. Simultaneously, the administrative requirements of a growing trading state outpaced royal household capacity. Both problems were addressed by the same king.

Mitraman (~100–65 BCE)

Mitraman — eighth holder of that regnal name — was a merchant king whose family interests were concentrated in haritar. He understood from personal experience both the logic of armed escorts and the danger of allowing that logic to run unchecked.

The Naval Consolidation

Mitraman's proposal was a genuine exchange: each guild contributes to a common naval fund; the crown provides standardised armed escort for all merchant ships; private armed ships are retired or transferred to royal command. Land grants near Mahishmati compensated families who surrendered naval assets. Crucially, Mitraman also surrendered the royal customs exemption — applying the same tax rules to himself. The Gupta family — the most powerful holdout — was brought in through the marriage of Mitraman's daughter Princess Suveera into the Gupta family. This moment is the canonical founding of the Royal Avantian Navy.

Amravati University

Main article: Amravati University

Mitraman endowed an existing ashram at Amravati (~90 BCE) — originally training land surveyors, customs agents, and administrators. Contact with Satavahana and Tamil Sangam scholars over the following fifty years transformed it into a genuine university.

The Succession Crisis

Mitraman's only son and crown prince, Aryaman, died in an accident at sea. Two nephews remained: Srimat (elder, stronger hereditary claim) and Sumant (younger, more capable administrator). Guild families began quietly aligning. The fragile unity of the naval consolidation began to soften.

The Conclave of Twelve

Mitraman convened a formal assembly of all twelve founding families and proposed a new succession law: the king nominates his crown prince; the other eleven family heads must formally approve; all twelve then swear collective allegiance to the approved prince. This permanently altered the constitutional relationship between crown and families. Mitraman nominated Sumant. The Conclave approved — the vote was not unanimous, but the Archive records no dissenting names.

The Srimat Question

Srimat did not start a civil war. Every Avantian historian has noted this. Passed over for a younger rival, he had every incentive toward violence. He chose against it. The most persistent tradition holds that he settled eventually in the Satavahana kingdom. The Archive contains no final communication from him. The contrast between his departure and the chaos a civil war would have produced is noted explicitly in the chronicles of Sumant's coronation. The contrast between Srimat and Manikarnin's older son — who accepted being passed over at the 1584 yajna with what the chronicles call "constitutional grace" — is a recurring theme in Avantian historical writing, attributed not to individual virtue but to institutional maturity.

Sumant (~65–20 BCE)

Sumant — ninth holder of that regnal name — ruled approximately forty-five years. His reign is characterised as the great work of consolidation: embedding institutions deeply enough that even bad successors could not undo them. The Augustus parallel is explicit in Avantian historical writing.

The Mitragan

Sumant formalised the twelve founding families into the Mitragan — the Council of Friends. The king owes the Mitragan consultation; the Mitragan owes the king collective allegiance. The Mitragan meets formally at the annual state yajna — a deliberate weaving of the political and the religious, ensuring institutional renewal before the assembled community every year without exception. No meeting of the Mitragan has been missed since Sumant established the annual obligation.

The Mercantile Council

Sumant formalised the guild network into the Mercantile Council. Its first major test came immediately: Rome's absorption of Egypt in 30 BCE transformed the Indian Ocean trade landscape. Sumant placed the question of how to manage the Parthia-to-Rome pivot before the Council rather than deciding himself — establishing the precedent that major commercial policy decisions are made with the merchant community rather than for it.

The Sovereignty Proclamation

Sumant formally proclaimed sovereignty across the entire archipelago and extended the coastal defense signal chain to every inhabited island. The proclamation was accompanied by a ceremonial circuit by the Royal Navy — the first time the full fleet sailed in formation around all thirty-seven islands. The Archive records that the circuit took eleven days.

The Dvaya Legacy

Medieval scholars coined Dvaya to describe the two reigns as a single epoch. Neither reign made complete sense without the other: Mitraman created the institutions; Sumant embedded them. The annual state yajna incorporates a commemorative reading from both reigns' chronicles, interleaved — as if the two kings still speak to each other across time.

Institutions Tested

The Amravati Archive records at least three significant attempts by post-Dvaya kings to circumvent the constitutional arrangements — a customs exemption attempt (Mercantile Council refused, king capitulated), a Mitragan bypass attempt (families refused allegiance oath, king capitulated within three months), and an Amravati defunding (university survived on private endowments for seven years; scholars published their most important work during those years).
