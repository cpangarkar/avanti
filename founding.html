The Founding of Avanti

~305 BCE · The Mauryan Expedition · Vitihotra and the Twelve Families

The Founding Expedition
Date11 Shukla Shuddh Kartik, 17th year of Chandragupta's reign (~305 BCE)
Departure pointBarygaza (present-day Bharuch), Gujarat
Vessels3 dhows
Personnel~1,500 settlers
LeaderVitihotra, prince of mainland Avanti
SponsorEmperor Chandragupta Maurya
First settlementAvantika (traditional)
Original familiesVijayin, Chandra, Pradyotin, Vardhan, Parashar
Second wave familiesGupta, Yadav, Suryavanshin, Acharya, Vyas, Manikandin, Somnathin
The founding of Avanti rests on a combination of Mauryan administrative records, the unbroken royal chronicles held at the Amravati Archive, and a body of oral tradition — particularly among communities descended from the original artisan and farming contingents — that has never been fully committed to writing. Where sources diverge, this article notes the disagreement.

The Mauryan Context

By the early years of Chandragupta Maurya's reign, the Avantian archipelago was not unknown. Sailors making the passage between the Gulf of Khambhat and the southern Arabian coast had long used the islands as a storm shelter and watering point. What they reported back — fertile land, no habitation, unknown mineral deposits glimpsed in the mountain faces — was enough to interest an emperor whose strategic imagination ran well beyond the subcontinent.

Chandragupta's method was characteristically indirect. Rather than mounting a royal expedition, he issued a challenge to his vassals: travel to these islands, spend a year, claim the land in my name, and find something of value. The reward would be imperial favour and the fruits of whatever could be extracted. The risk would be entirely the vassal's own.

Vitihotra

The man who rose to the challenge was Vitihotra, younger brother of King Udravya of the mainland kingdom of Avanti — a prosperous state in west-central India from which the islands would eventually take their name. Vitihotra was clever, ambitious, and entirely clear-eyed about the arithmetic of being a second son. Udravya had no children yet, which meant Vitihotra was still technically in line for the throne — a situation that would resolve itself, one way or another, within a generation. Chandragupta's challenge was, at minimum, an adventure. At best, it was a kingdom of his own.

The emperor's blessing carried something more important than sentiment: it carried funds, imperial commercial networks, and the authority to recruit across Mauryan territory. With these, Vitihotra assembled a contingent of approximately 1,500 people — not a military force but a complete functioning community. Warriors, blacksmiths, miners, merchants, farmers, carpenters, cooks, physicians (vaidyas), priests, and their families. Five complete family units — the Vijayin, Chandra, Pradyotin, Vardhan, and Parashar clans — formed the social backbone of the expedition. They departed Barygaza on 11 Shukla Shuddh Kartik in the 17th year of Chandragupta's reign, the date preserved in both the Mauryan administrative record and the Amravati chronicle tradition.

Vitihotra's authority derived entirely from Chandragupta's commission. He owed nothing to his brother's kingdom. The mainland Avanti was the origin of a name and nothing more.

The First Year

Landfall

The precise location of the first landfall is one of the genuinely unresolved questions of Avantian historiography. The traditional account, preserved in the royal chronicles, places it at the estuary of the Mugdha river on the western coast — the site later occupied by the Pushpeshwar temple, whose founding is accordingly traced to the first days of settlement. Several historians have argued that the northwestern islands would have been a more natural first landfall given the prevailing direction of approach from Barygaza.

A competing tradition, and in the view of most modern historians the more plausible one, places the landfall at the estuary of the Aparganga — supported by the legend, recorded in multiple independent sources, of Vitihotra personally directing the digging of irrigation canals near the site of present-day Avantika. The Aparganga estuary is the logical location for a settlement requiring immediate freshwater canal infrastructure. The debate has not been formally resolved, and both traditions maintain scholarly defenders.

Survival and Exploration

The first year was not a struggle for survival in the sense the early European colonial chronicles record. The archipelago lay within the same geographic and climatic zone the settlers had known in India. They could grow rice and vegetables immediately. The land was fertile, the rains reliable, the sea abundant. What the contingent could not do — and what everything depended on — was find anything worth reporting back to an emperor.

The entire first year was consumed by systematic exploration: search expeditions through the island ranges, the river valleys, the coastal formations. Warriors and miners worked alongside each other under Vitihotra's personal direction. The settlements at Avantika were functional but provisional; everyone understood that the value of the enterprise depended on what could be extracted and sent back to Barygaza.

The Lapis Discovery

It was not until the very end of the first sailing season — when the window for sending a ship back to India was almost closed — that a mining expedition in the eastern ranges found what they were looking for: a brilliant blue vein running through the face of a cliff. Similar formations were visible in several locations across the main island range and on what would later be named Pushkaradiu. The contingent was uncertain what they had found. One member — a merchant who had handled trade goods from the northwest — thought the colour and texture resembled lapis lazuli beads he had seen in Barygaza. They gathered as many samples as they could carry.

A small contingent was dispatched back to India with the samples and a report. The rest remained. The year's obligation to the emperor had been met. Whether to stay longer was now a question each family answered for itself. Most stayed.

The Second Wave

The lapis samples reached Barygaza and caused immediate commercial excitement. What the archipelago held was not a modest deposit but a significant one — and the blue vein would prove to be only one of several extraordinary resources the islands contained. Chandragupta moved quickly. New investment was authorised: more miners, shipbuilders, and the merchant capital to support them.

The second wave arrived within the first two years of the original landing. Seven further family clans came as part of this organised investment — the Gupta, Yadav, Suryavanshin, Acharya, Vyas, Manikandin, and Somnathin families — bringing the founding clans to the canonical twelve. They came with resources and commercial expertise the original five could not have provided alone. Any sense of precedence the first families might have held dissolved quickly in the practical reality that the enterprise needed all twelve. The islands that the first wave had survived on instinct and hard work, the second wave helped make productive and profitable.

The Mauryan commercial arrangement was formalised at this point: anything of value extracted from the islands must route first through Barygaza. The archipelago was, formally, a Mauryan resource. Vitihotra governed it as Chandragupta's man.

The Carpenter Legend

The Amravati Archive contains no written record of the following account. It exists entirely in oral tradition — carried for over two thousand years primarily within the communities descended from the artisan and farming contingents of the original expedition, passed down as a founding story of a particular kind of justice.

In the first year of the settlement, a senior member of the expedition — described in the oral tradition as Vitihotra's own uncle, a kshatriya — struck and injured a carpenter badly enough to incapacitate him. Under the legal norms of the time, injury to a sudra by a kshatriya carried only a financial penalty. A fine would have been paid and the matter closed.

Vitihotra refused to apply that precedent. His reasoning, as the tradition records it, was not principled abstraction — it was entirely communal and practical. In a settlement of 1,500 people in an unknown archipelago, every person was indispensable. A carpenter who could not work meant structures that could not be built, boats that could not be repaired, tools that could not be made. Injury to one was injury to all. The punishment Vitihotra imposed on his uncle was commensurate with this reasoning, and it was public.

What the oral tradition preserved was not primarily the punishment but the principle: that in Avanti, a carpenter's value equalled a warrior's, because survival depended on both equally. This was never enacted as law. It was never written down. But it circulated in the sudra-descended communities as a live precedent — a story that had been invoked in disputes about how a craftsman or farmer should be treated, and could be invoked again. The degree to which this legend created Avantian egalitarianism or merely reflected a social reality already forming is a question historians have not resolved. What is not in dispute is that the story functioned as a social fact for the communities who carried it — a claim on fairness that existed independently of what any written law said.

Vitihotra's Place in the Historical Record

The Amravati chronicles describe Vitihotra as the organiser of every significant early enterprise: the settlement camps, the farming rotations, the search expeditions, the canal works at Avantika. He is not described as a military leader — there was no military challenge in these years — but as an administrator of unusual energy and personal involvement. The image that comes through consistently is of a man who worked alongside his community rather than directing it from above.

Whether this image is historically precise or retrospectively constructed, it shaped the Avantian understanding of legitimate authority. The tradition that a ruler earns authority through service to the community — not merely by holding it — runs from the carpenter legend through the Dvaya era's institutional design through the Rajadharma's dharmic framing of kingship. Vitihotra did not write any of this down. But the community he built carried the idea forward.

His connection to the mainland kingdom of Avanti is not mentioned again in any source after the departure from Barygaza. His brother Udravya, his family's throne, the world he left — all of it recedes. What remained was the settlement, and the name it carried.

The Mauryan Period (~305–180 BCE)

For five or six generations, the archipelago functioned as a productive Mauryan dependency. Lapis lazuli and the early fruits of haritar dye production flowed through Barygaza. The commercial arrangement was profitable for all parties, including the island families, who accumulated substantial wealth within the Mauryan trading framework.

The disgruntlement that eventually produced independence did not exist in Vitihotra's generation, nor in the next. It grew slowly — in the third and fourth generations — as families who had built the archipelago with their own capital and labour began to experience the Mauryan commercial monopoly as a constraint rather than a partnership. The progressive weakening and mismanagement of Ashoka's successors accelerated this: Mauryan authority in the outlying territories frayed, and the island families found themselves maintaining an obligation to an empire that could no longer deliver the protections and market access that had made the obligation worthwhile.

The declaration of independence by Vishnuhotra — a descendant of the founding king, bearing a new regnal name in the family tradition — came approximately 180 BCE. By that point it was less a rebellion than a formal recognition of a reality that had existed for a generation. For the history of the period that followed, see The Dvaya Era.

This article covers the founding expedition and Mauryan period (~305–180 BCE). The period between independence (180 BCE) and the Dvaya era (~100 BCE) — first independent kings, early commercial expansion, initial Satavahana contact — is under development.
