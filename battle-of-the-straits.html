Battle of the Straits (1571)

18–19 September 1571 · Decisive Avantian victory · End of Portuguese occupation

Battle of the Straits (1571)

LocationAvantian Archipelago — northern and western straits

Background

Following the Massacre of Kusukhatu and the failure of diplomacy at Goa, Viceroy Dom Luís de Ataíde assembled a fleet larger than anything previously deployed in the Arabian Sea. The Portuguese expected a repeat of 1549: superior firepower in open water would overwhelm Avantian resistance. This expectation was formed entirely from 1549 experience and did not account for twelve years of secret rearmament.

The Portuguese Fleet

Thirty-two ships in two squadrons: an eastern squadron from Portuguese India approaching from the northeast, and a western squadron from Aden commanded by Dom Almeida approaching from the west. The strategy was a simultaneous pincer, preventing Avantian concentration against either force. Portuguese ships carried standard Estado armament — high-sided carracks and galleons with bronze cannon, experienced crews, decades of Indian Ocean combat experience.

Avantian Preparations

The Signal Chain

The coastal signal chain — observation posts on every headland and outer island, spaced within visual range — tracked the assembling Portuguese fleet for weeks. Fire and smoke signals relayed information to Rakshika naval base within one to two hours. Avanti knew the size of both squadrons and their likely timing. The Avantian fleet was distributed throughout the archipelago in concentrated groups, ready to converge on engagement zones that Boogman and Vardhan had selected — positioned near strait approaches whose complex navigation advantaged defenders over unfamiliar attackers.

Tactical Doctrine

Boogman's doctrine rested on a single insight: Portuguese naval superiority was a function of open-water conditions. In the straits and shallows of the Avantian archipelago, Portuguese advantages became liabilities — height meant more draft and harder manoeuvre in confined channels; formation discipline broke against unfamiliar navigation; and the sixteen named straits, memorised by every Avantian sailor since childhood, became lethal for Portuguese pilots who had never charted them. Fire ships were prepared for deployment in the narrowest straits.

The Commanders

Jannick Boogman commanded the eastern engagement — pressing the Indian squadron in the northern straits. His tactical instinct and aggressiveness, forcing engagements before Portuguese commanders could adapt, was decisive throughout the night.

Dinakar Vardhan commanded the western approaches, bringing intimate lifetime knowledge of every strait, shoal, current, and tidal pattern in the western archipelago. He deployed fire ships in channels he had sailed since boyhood. His geographic mastery of the battlefield complemented Boogman's tactical instinct — a partnership that neither could have equalled alone.

Wian Thyssen served as senior gunnery officer, moving between engagements where his crews' discipline was most needed. The gun crews he had trained for twelve years maintained rate of fire and accuracy through hours of close-range combat in darkness and smoke.

The Battle (18–19 September 1571)

The Portuguese eastern squadron entered Avantian waters from the northeast in the late afternoon of 18 September. The first engagements were not the open-water artillery duels the Portuguese expected, but close-quarters fights in strait approaches where Avantian ships appeared to retreat and drew the Portuguese into uncharged channels. The western squadron, approaching simultaneously, encountered Vardhan's forces in the western straits as darkness fell.

The battle lasted through the night. The Portuguese kept losing ships — to cannon fire at unexpected ranges, to fire ships in straits too narrow to avoid, to Avantian vessels appearing from channels not shown on Portuguese charts. The two-directional approach became a liability in the darkness: the eastern and western squadrons could not coordinate, and Boogman and Vardhan exploited every gap. Avantian losses were not negligible, but Avanti had prepared for them — sufficient ships and trained crews that losses did not break momentum.

By dawn on 19 September it was over. Twenty-one Portuguese ships were sunk or destroyed. The remaining eleven were damaged. Admiral Dom Almeida formally surrendered just before noon on 19 September 1571.

Treaty of Mormugao

Dom Luís de Ataíde accepted terms. The Treaty of Mormugao established: Portugal vacates all Avantian possessions permanently; the cartaz system is abolished; Portugal uses no force against Avantian merchants anywhere in the Indian Ocean; Avantian traders receive guaranteed rights in Estado ports; all Portuguese trade between India and East Africa routes through Avanti. The most revealing provision was what Avanti did not demand: no punitive taxes, no tribute. The principle asserted was the freedom of the open ocean — the right of all merchants to use the sea without paying ransom to any power possessing warships.

Legacy

Boogman and Thyssen settled permanently in Avanti, took Avantian wives, and are buried in the archipelago. The cities bearing their names stand near the site of the Portuguese settlements they helped erase. They are taught in Avantian schools not as foreign mercenaries but as Avantians who happened to be born elsewhere — the most famous expression of a tradition, rooted in the founding-era openness and absence of rigid caste, of absorbing contributors regardless of origin.

Dinakar Vardhan was confirmed thirteen years later as the first Navadhyaksha under the Rajadharma of 1584. He is regarded as the finest Avantian naval commander of his era. The joint decision of 1571 — king and full Mitragan together — increased Mitragan approval power over military decisions, codified in the Rajadharma.
