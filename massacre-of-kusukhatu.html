Massacre of Kusukhatu

29 May 1571 · Pushkaradiu · Most contested event in Avantian history

The Massacre of Kusukhatu is the most morally contested event in Avantian history. This article presents the historical record alongside the full range of scholarly and public debate across four centuries.

The Portuguese Occupation (1549–1571)

The Portuguese Estado da India extended its cartaz system to Avantian waters in the late 1540s. When King Manikarnin and the Mitragan refused to recognise Portuguese jurisdiction, the Estado responded militarily. Portuguese forces defeated the Avantian fleet in 1549 and landed troops. The terms extracted were comprehensive: two permanent bases on Pushkaradiu and the right to levy cartaz taxes on all ships entering or leaving Avantian waters. For twenty-two years the cartaz operated — every merchant ship paid the Portuguese levy. The economic damage was substantial; the political humiliation total.

Secret Rearmament (1559–1571)

The pathare families proposed the solution: hire Dutch sailors with direct experience of Portuguese naval technology. Jannick Boogman (Dutch naval captain) and Wian Thyssen (master gunnery officer) were hired in 1559. Thyssen supervised the casting of heavier bronze cannon, designed reinforced gunports, and trained gun crews to European standards. Boogman developed the tactical doctrine: refuse open-water battle; draw Portuguese ships into the straits where navigational unfamiliarity became lethal. By May 1571, both judged the fleet ready. Manikarnin and the full Mitragan agreed.

The Villages of Kusukhatu

The two Portuguese settlements on Pushkaradiu's northern coast had grown over twenty-two years into self-contained communities — each with a stone church, a school, an army garrison of approximately sixty soldiers, a harbour, and a mixed population of Portuguese civilians, Avantian dock workers, Arab merchants, and Indian traders.

The Orders

The orders issued by King Manikarnin and ratified by the Mitragan are preserved in the Amravati Archive in what is believed to be the original signed document. They specified: every adult Portuguese male above the age of fourteen was to be killed; Portuguese women and children taken prisoner unharmed; non-Portuguese inhabitants left unmolested; Portuguese ships prevented from reaching open water.

Execution

Two Avantian contingents moved simultaneously against both villages in the early hours of 29 May — from land and sea. Both villages were secured within hours. Seven Portuguese ships were captured. By dawn both settlements were in Avantian hands. The Amravati Archive records the operation without euphemism. Later chronicles consistently use the term vadha — slaughter. No subsequent Avantian historical tradition has disputed what happened, though every tradition has disputed what it meant.

Embassy to Goa

A royal ambassador was dispatched to Viceroy Dom Luís de Ataíde offering terms: vacate all Avantian possessions, renounce the cartaz system. Dom Luís — who had successfully defended Goa against a Bijapur siege with a fraction of the required garrison — declined without extended negotiation. He began assembling a fleet. The embassy returned empty-handed. For the military history of what followed, see Battle of the Straits (1571).

Aftermath

The prisoners were returned following the Treaty of Mormugao. The seven commandeered ships were retained. The settlements at Kusukhatu were not rebuilt — the site became the Avantian port city eventually known as Thyssen, named after the Dutch gunnery officer who had made the victory possible. The name was chosen deliberately. The non-Portuguese civilian population continued living there under Avantian administration.

The Moral Debate

The Traditional Defence

Three arguments: the proportionality argument (twenty-two years of occupation exhausted every diplomatic alternative); the military necessity argument (leaving armed soldiers alive would have produced far greater total casualties); and the outcome argument (the Treaty of Mormugao established the freedom of the open ocean, benefiting millions who never knew the dead of Kusukhatu).

Medieval Dissent — Anantavarman

The historian Anantavarman (14th century) — writing two centuries before the Massacre — argued in Rajadharma and its Limits that a state which kills unarmed civilians to achieve strategic ends concedes something it cannot recover regardless of the justice of its cause. His formulation was never quite answered. It is assigned reading at Amravati to this day.

The Postmodern Turn

The generation of Avantians born after 1970 — the first to grow up entirely within democratic Avanti, without living memory of colonial threat — encountered Kusukhatu through global human rights discourse and postcolonial scholarship. The result was a significant current of opinion that the Massacre was simply wrong. Not strategically mistaken. Not a necessary evil. A moral failure the Avantian state had never properly reckoned with.

The Kusukhatu Apology Movement

The Kusukhatu Apology Movement (emerging early 1990s) argues the King of Avanti should issue a formal apology to Portugal. Its annual commemoration on 29 May is held at the site of the old Portuguese settlements in the city of Thyssen — with deliberate and contested irony. A moderate wing argues an apology is compatible with pride in the outcome. A radical wing argues the entire framing of the Massacre as necessary for liberation is nationalist mythology. Portugal has never formally requested an apology.

The Royal Position

No King of Avanti has ever apologized to Portugal for the Massacre of Kusukhatu. The royal position — consistently maintained across four reigns — is that the events of 1571 cannot be judged by removing them from their historical context. Public opinion surveys consistently show a majority opposed to an apology, though the majority shrinks significantly among voters under thirty-five. Both sides argue from within the same Avantian value system. The Amravati Archive contains both positions, argued with equal conviction, in every century since 1571. The debate has never been resolved. Perhaps it should not be.
